Jannik Hofstetter

17.10.2005

I was never someone who simply accepts things as they are. When I do something, I want to understand why it works. That idea has been with me since early on. In strategy games, for example, it was never about clicking faster—it was about recognizing patterns in my opponents’ behavior and figuring out how to react. To me, that was proof: when you understand how something is built, you can influence it.

That continued at school. I tried to describe happiness as a formula—built from subjective impressions, objective conditions, and environmental influences. The goal wasn’t to solve life mathematically, but to understand which factors truly shape our state of mind. With love, it was different: I analyzed the process of love and the dynamics behind it—how people arrive at certain thoughts, decisions, and bonds—which drew me into neurobiology.

I looked at systems and algorithms the same way. With the Rubik’s Cube, I wasn’t interested in the colorful end result, but in the logic behind the moves—why certain sequences reliably lead to a specific state. The same principle fascinated me with the Collatz-Problem: a simple rule that generates complex, seemingly chaotic paths. And as soon as you plot such sequences on a coordinate system, you start seeing structures where there seemed to be only randomness.

In the end, there’s a common thread: I like observing how things work and look for patterns I can use. Whether it’s in code, data, or real behavior—once you understand the system, you can influence and change it. My name’s Jannik Hofstetter, welcome to my personal website.

– Hofstetter, 2025
